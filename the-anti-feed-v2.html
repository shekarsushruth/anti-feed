Rabbithole

1
Turritopsis dohrnii — the immortal jellyfish

There's a jellyfish the size of your pinky nail that is, in a real biological sense, immortal. When Turritopsis dohrnii is injured, starving, or just old, it reverts to its juvenile polyp form, rewinding its own clock and starting over. Unless something eats it, it can do this indefinitely.

The mechanism breaks a rule you were taught. Most animal cells are locked in. This jellyfish performs transdifferentiation: specialized adult cells un-specialize, then re-specialize into entirely different types. Picture a butterfly that could turn itself back into a caterpillar.

Why it matters: this is the exact cellular reprogramming cancer and regenerative-medicine labs chase — convincing specialized cells to change identity on command is the holy grail behind regrowing a damaged heart. This jellyfish does it natively.

The catch: "immortal" only means it doesn't die of aging. In the ocean, most get eaten as plankton long before they use their reset. Less "unkillable," more "has no expiry date."

Psychology

2
Choice blindness & the confabulating brain

A lot of what feels like deciding is actually explaining a decision your brain already made. In "choice blindness" experiments, people pick a face, are secretly handed back the one they didn't pick, and then confidently explain why they chose it — defending a preference that was never theirs.

The split-brain version is starker: the verbal half of the brain invents sincere reasons for actions the other half took. The takeaway isn't "you're broken" — it's that certainty about your own motives is itself a story your mind tells. Worth holding lightly next time you're sure why you did something.

Well-replicated across choice-blindness and split-brain literatures.

Implementation intentions

One of the most robust behavior-change findings, and almost embarrassingly simple. "I'll edit more" fails because the when/where is undecided, so you decide in the moment — tired, feed right there. Implementation intentions fix it in advance: "If it's 9am and I've had coffee, then I open the Resolve timeline before anything else."

The mechanism: you pre-load the trigger so the situation cues the action, instead of summoning willpower each time. For your procrastination, the move is to name the cue, not the intention — tie the task to something that already happens in your day.

Gollwitzer's implementation intentions — large evidence base, strong real-world effects.

Philosophy

1
Parfit's teleporter & personal identity

Derek Parfit's setup: a teleporter scans every atom of you, destroys the original, and builds a perfect copy on Mars from new matter — same memories, same scar. The copy wakes sure it's you and lives your life. Did you travel to Mars, or die in the scanner while a stranger with your memories took over?

It sharpens fast. Suppose the machine doesn't destroy the original — now there are two of you, and they can't both be you. Yet the Mars copy is exactly as continuous with your past as it was a second ago. So whatever makes "you" you, it isn't the thing you instinctively reach for.

Parfit's radical move: maybe the question has no answer because it was never a real fact. What matters is psychological continuity — and once you've described that fully, there's no further fact about whether the future person "is really you." Identity is a label over a process, not the deep thing.

Why it's not abstract: you're already in a slow teleporter. Your atoms mostly weren't there years ago; you're tied to your ten-year-old self only by an unbroken chain of overlapping memory and matter — the same kind of chain the machine provides. If that's enough across decades, why not across the scanner? The thing you fear about the teleporter might be quietly true of ordinary life.
